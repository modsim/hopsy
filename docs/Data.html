class hopsy.Data¶
class hopsy.ChainData¶
hopsy.compute_acceptance_rate(data: hopsy.Data) → numpy.ndarray[numpy.float64[m, 1]]¶

Compute the average acceptance rate of the chains in data. Acceptance rates are returned in an m x 1 column vector, where m is the number of chains stored in data.

The acceptance rate is actually also logged after every chain iteration and stored in the ChainData, but this initializes the acceptance_rate field inside the Data object and thus allows to discard the samples.

hopsy.compute_effective_sample_size(data: hopsy.Data) → numpy.ndarray[numpy.float64[m, 1]]¶

Compute the effective sample size of the chains in data. The effective sample size is computed for every dimension individually and is then returned in an m x 1 column vector, where m is the dimension of the states.

hopsy.compute_expected_squared_jump_distance(data: hopsy.Data) → numpy.ndarray[numpy.float64[m, 1]]¶

Compute the expected squared jump distance of the chains in data. The expected squared jump distance is computed for every chain individually and is then returned in an m x 1 column vector, where m is the number of chains stored in data.

hopsy.compute_potential_scale_reduction_factor(data: hopsy.Data) → numpy.ndarray[numpy.float64[m, 1]]¶

Compute the potential scale reduction factor (also known as R-hat) of the chains in data. The potential scale reduction factor is computed for every dimension individually and is then returned in an m x 1 column vector, where m is the dimension of the states.

hopsy.compute_total_time_taken(data: hopsy.Data) → numpy.ndarray[numpy.float64[m, 1]]¶

Compute the total time taken of the chains in data. Times are returned in an m x 1 column vector, where m is the number of chains stored in data.

Timestamps are actually also logged after every chain iteration and stored in the ChainData, so this function just takes the difference of the last and first timestamp. It also initializes the total_time_taken field inside the Data object and thus allows to discard the samples.
